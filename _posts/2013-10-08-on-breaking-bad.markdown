So, [Breaking Bad](http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0903747/) has finally come to an end. It's definitely great drama, and I liked the series all through. But I have to admit my favorite TV series still continues to be [The Wire](http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0306414/) though it seems less rated than BB.

In my eyes, The Wire tackled a larger canvas, way more characters and a lot more credulous than almost fantasy-like narration of BB. Not to mention the way it tries to put a complex socio-economic problem like drugs in perspective and still tell a gripping tale in the process.

But the premise of BB still continues to bother me - that an underpaid high-school teacher who couldn't afford his cancer care goes rogue and becomes a meth cook. Of course, in the final episode he does agree he did it all "for himself", but the justification for his behavior through the series has been that he's trying to provide for his family.

The theme rather repeats itself in the last season, with Mike, the muscle behind Fring's operations trying to provide for his grand daughter. And in both Walter (the protagonist) and Mike's cases, that's a serious amount of money to leave. In my experience, leaving an indecent amount of money to your children will only help them become lazy and irresponsible. I think every parent or caretaker should try to give the best education possible to their wards, and let them grow their own wings after that. I think the result would be much healthier individuals after they grow up.

Other than that, the main premise - that a high-school teacher not able to afford healthcare - still bothers me. Of course you might have seen the [cartoons](http://www.salon.com/2013/09/13/amazing_comic_strip_shows_why_breaking_bad_is_only_possible_in_the_united_states/) and I will betray my socialist leanings here, but if the state provides for good healthcare, education and basic incomes, I think a lot of social ills that come through such insecurities can be more effectively dealt with. Of course some economists and right-leaning thinkers would like to say it can't be done, but we do see countries like Canada and the Nordic ones who have come a long way on this path with considerable success. The question is, why isn't this considered a serious goal to pursue for every government? Why isn't more research going to the viability of this model?
